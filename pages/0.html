000.04
Because this essay is about digital images, and because it was produced as a digital image, it ought to be read as a digital image.
The above instance about Street Fighter is not an exception to this because those efforts towards a non-digital procedure were designed to aide my understanding of my participation in this final digital image; it was a gesture towards, and all for the sake of, this digital image.
 And though I appreciate the time my reader gives to any new instance of this image — I am grateful to be a part of any new image — I do have a preference. This preference stems from more than an author’s pedantic desire for consistency through topic and reader experience. The image I seek to fulfil here, with my reader, cannot transpire and cannot be experienced with a printed page. It can only be read and viewed and participated in, in the fullest sense, with a swiping thumb suspended over a smartphone or with a hand resting on a mouse. For, this image is interactive, as I will partially explain, after these four paragraphs about anthropology.
000.05
Anthropology has always been more than ethnography. If ethnography looks like ‘establishing rapport, selecting informants, transcribing texts, taking genealogies, mapping fields, keeping a diary, and so on’ (Geertz 1973:6), or if it is something less specific like Lévi–Strauss (1955/2011:17) ‘proposing to tell the story of my expeditions’, these descriptions of ethnography do not capture what anthropology is. This is not to say ethnographic practice, some form of participant observation directed towards the human experience, is not an essential part of anthropology, only that anthropology does not end there. Anthropologists hold their discipline as an organon which encompasses ethnography; anthropology applies/employs ethnographic practice for theoretical, scientific or philosophical ends. Implicit in the articulation of an instance of anthropology is the assumption that this practice is more than mere ethnography, yet it cannot let ethnography go.
000.06
How then to describe anthropology? Ethnography allows anthropology to resist definitions tied to any singular method or aspect. Geertz (1973:6) did not want ethnographic practice to be mistaken for anthropology itself; he saw ‘these things, techniques and received procedures’ as insufficient for a definition of anthropology. More recently, beyond an ‘elaborate venture in... “thick description”’ (Geertz 1973:6) we have further supplementals to the method, rounding out and complicating anthropology’s understanding of itself: ‘Anthropology... is philosophy with the people in’ (Ingold 2014:para.1); ‘Anthropology [can be]... the theory/practice of the permanent decolonization of thought’ (Viveiros de Castro 2014:40); ‘Anthropology is a venture in cultural transformation’ (Pandian 2019:6), etc. But these are only local definitions designed to serve the author’s projects and together represent a lack of agreement. These authors want anthropology to be more than practice, more than mere ethnography.
000.07
They each give anthropology some philosophical (Ingold), theoretical (Viveiros de Castro), or political (Pandian) spin while preserving, or at least leaving room for, traditional modes of data collection; no one negates the practice of ethnography. When Pandian (2019:8) frames anthropology as a means of ‘sustaining contrary ways of imagining and inhabiting the contemporary world’, he associates these features of the anthropological project with its methodological heritage and resists ‘disavowing this heritage altogether’. Ethnographic images of the master of the fishing spear’s live burial (Lienhardt 1961:300), an Azande consultation with a poison oracle (Evans-Pritchard 1937/1976:127), a Ndembu circumcision rite (Turner 1967/1982:151), etc., represent a by-product of the anthropological process still being generated today via/as images of Yukaghir hunting practices (Willerslev 2007), the process of exoplanet astronomy and place-making (Messeri 2016), ‘expanding ethnography to reach beyond the human’ (Kohn 2013:6), etc. These images of the human, their production, documentation, analysis, comparison, theorization, speculation and so on, are an essential element of what anthropologists do.
000.08
This is how contemporary anthropology still finds itself valuing the ethnographic image and despite new definitions still regards itself as what I call an image generating technology. Though much anthropology would offer alternative descriptions of itself, this is an aspect of my local definition. It is a definition that will be further justified but anyone who does not accept this feature of anthropology will not accept the following thesis; they will not accept what videogames have to say here, about anthropology.
000.09
The anthropologist interested in videogames has a different relationship to the phenomena which are to be represented, described and thought with, because the human event in question is already a multi-layered image. This fictional image of an enhanced human, Master Chief, aka John-117 (recipient of the SPARTAN-II augmentation procedures, 9 March 2525), killing Banished Unggoy Grunt and Kig-yar Jackal mercenaries with a plasma cannon (circa 28 May 2560), is a perfect reproduction of the image that transpired on my computer monitor 22 April 2022 (open and press play)
(Staten et al. 2021). This image not only conveys the exact arrangement of pixels constituting the human image-event in question but delivers a representation of each of my decisions on 22 April 2022 and the culmination of the many prior decisions of the development team which contributed to that singular outcome. Here I am comparing the videogame’s capacity to generate human images to anthropology’s via ethnography. In defence of the latter, we could say that the videogame image leaves no room for ‘thick description’ (Geertz 1973:6) because of the inherent coalescence of image and event in the medium; the event itself is an image leaving no thickness to describe, we already have all the data. Therefore, this competing practice of distorted representations of the human ought to be ignored criticised or separated from more compelling human assemblages like online virtual worlds, which can transpire in instances of online gaming but which ought to be separated from the videogame image itself.
000.10
Contrarily, what follows is primarily concerned with the videogame image as a human event, its production and consumption, its inherent (non-)violence and creativity. This entails a different understanding of the videogame image than what I presented in the previous paragraph, which in turn opens up a different understanding of the anthropological image. Herein lies the real opportunity for anthropology (and videogames) in this forcing of the discipline to confront and do something with such an unnatural topic. Not only does the videogame image usher anthropological discourse towards new modes — beyond capturing more detail of the human experience we are pressed, in a style Deleuze and Guattari (1991/2015) would welcome, to work with the phenomena to generate new concepts — not only does it contribute to Viveiros de Castro’s (2014:40) ‘decolonization of thought’ but a ‘democracy of thought’ (Laruelle 1996/2013:69) because the anthropological ethnographic image and the data it rests on to qualify itself have no authority over the videogame image and both images are made equal participants in this new image. The videogame image, and as a consequence the anthropological image with it, are not instances of the human to be described but invitations to enter new human events, new images, of which this project is one. Another more simple but less implicative way to say this is, because the videogame image is already an image and because that image implies features unique to software (it is easily accessible and infinitely reproducible (see Free Software and GNU/Linux)) we are spurred to think in new ways, to find a way to contribute something more than the recording. That is, not necessarily to thicken the image, to anthropologise it and make it fulfil the needs of the anthropological discipline’s project, but to find new ways into the human image itself as something we perform, to reframe it as a new human event, to generate a new anthropological image.
000.11
Many of the pages that follow, like the present one, contain images and short videos, captured from the videogames being discussed. They should be played and/or viewed in the instances where they occur in the text. They are, in one limited sense, perfect portrayals of the events in question. The images and concepts conveyed in the text are suspended together in a way that is dialogic. This allows for something more to be present in the image and prose together, through a kind of relationality, which would otherwise be impossible.
000.12
By playing each of the videos, this reader/user/viewer/player is producing an image which has not happened yet, collaboratively, with me. This is in the same way I produced the videos throughout, collaboratively with the developers of the videogames that are presented. And I have produced this image (as I said at the beginning) collaboratively with the developers of the software I used to write it.
