2 THE VIDEOGAME IMAGE, AS TRANSCENDENTAL

049
It is easy to approach the videogame image via the cinema, at least as a first step. This is because thinking with videogames through concepts owed to cinema entails language and ideas that are familiar to us. Though Galloway’s (2006:2) definition of videogames draws a clear distinction between the two mediums — ‘If photographs are images, and films are moving images, then video games are actions’ — he still relies on cinematographic language and gestures to explore what videogames are doing. For example, the first-person shooter is described as a POV shot, and Italian neorealism is evoked to urge videogames towards a social realism, focused on ‘narrative, not representation’ (Galloway 2006:39,75,80). When Atkins (2003:24) imagines some future day when ‘…perhaps, the computer game will even produce its À la Recherche du Temps Perdu or its Ulysses, its Casablanca or its Citizen Kane’, it is easy to see his point. When will the videogame produce, and (perhaps more importantly) be recognised for, the kind of penetrating reflections on life and the human condition commonly celebrated in the classics of literature and cinema? And this question assumes such classics will surely reveal themselves in time, for the videogame is assumed to be on a similar trajectory to other mediums born out of older technologies, most recently cinema.
050
And it is easy to regard many videogames as a kind of interactive film, especially when they are so often designed and presented in cinematic ways. Motion capture is now a stage of development many films and videogames share: the capturing and reproduction of movement for the sake of an image it has not yet become. Computer-generated images are not an essential part of videogames or films, but both have embraced that style of image-making. There is a clear difference in application. Videogames usually need the image of gameplay to be one continuous shot, so motion capture is reserved for those instances where videogames express what is unique about them the least: the cutscene. An exception could be interactive cutscenes, like the dance battle in Final Fantasy VII Remake (Nomura et al. 2020) or the torture scene in The Last of Us Part II (Druckmann et al. 2020). These are pulling the functions of the original cutscene, where videogames resemble film, into the realm of interactivity. These instances, especially when they are taken outside the broader context of the videogames they are a part of, can feel and look like watching a film that demands occasional button presses, to be sure we are paying attention.
051
Interactive or otherwise, cutscenes have been a part of videogames since we started traversing 3D worlds, at least since Super Mario 64 (Miyamoto 1996). There, we see the visuals of gameplay itself presenting a greater conceptual hurdle in the minds of developers and players than the game’s cutscenes. The opening cutscene attempts to provide a diegetic explanation for the new cinematic element of gameplay, namely an adjustable camera following the controllable character. It opens with a sweeping aerial shot of Peach’s castle, following one of the Lakitu Bros. — a turtle-like creature, floating on a cloud, holding a fishing rod with a camera attached to it. We see a pipe come out of the ground and the Lakitu Bro. circles around. It then cuts to a POV shot of the pipe from the Lakitu Bro.’s camera’s perspective,
, Mario jumps out of the pipe, and this is how we are introduced to the floating continuous shot following the controllable character in third-person videogames (Miyamoto 1996). When we move the camera around Mario, we are actually controlling one of the Lakitu Bros.
052
This was demonstrated to us in the first instance via the language of cinema, a language we already understood. What we see in Super Mario 64 has become the standard transition from cutscene to gameplay. The over-the-shoulder-shot pulls us into the image, it hands us the reigns or, more accurately, poses to the viewer a question and by answering that question the viewer becomes a player. We have come to expect this kind of shot, this transition where cinema is metamorphosing into a videogame. It is worth noting how Final Fantasy VII Remake (Nomura et al. 2020) plays with that expectation in its opening cutscene. We are eager to take control, and the camera teases us by first settling behind Biggs and then Barret before finally orienting us in relation to the game’s protagonist, Cloud.
(Nomura et al. 2020). Here, the cinema here is moving on the videogame’s terms, it cannot escape asking the question and in Final Fantasy VII Remake we see a creative evolution of how it is being asked.
053
Does this demonstrate how strongly cinema has framed our comprehension of new media or the immaturity of the videogame as a medium itself? A medium’s immaturity is what Deleuze (1983/2013:3) identified in early cinema, for
…things and people are always forced to conceal themselves, have to conceal themselves when they begin. What else could they do? They come into being within a set which no longer includes them and, in order not to be rejected have to project the characteristics which they retain in common with the set.
Thus, just as cinema only commenced as an imitation of natural perception before later evolving into the movement-image, Super Mario 64 had to present itself as an interactive cinema, not only because that is what was understandable to us but because that is what the videogame understood itself to have become.
054
But this association with cinema does not work in every respect. One major difference is that, in many ways, the videogame’s resemblance to cinema does not describe the medium’s genesis but one of its strivings. There were certainly 2D videogames before there were 3D ones. And while the medium commenced with the manipulation of symbols and shapes in the forms of Pong (Alcorn 1972) and Pac-Man (Iwatani and Ishimura 1980; see Rodgers 2020:3’10”), the pursuit of the spectacle of photo-realism has led the videogame to resemble the movement-image of cinema more closely. Regardless of what we classify to be the first true videogame, and it is not Deleuze’s (1983/2013) style (and especially not Bergson’s (1907/2007), as we will see) to draw clear distinctions like that anyway, the cinematic videogame was an event for the medium. It was the beginning of a new form of encountering and participating in images — the event of the videogame image had become something new.
055
Videogames did not evolve out of cinema, but the illusions of movement in videogames have been developed with the cinema in mind. Cinema, while absent from most definitions of, or origin stories for, the medium of videogames, has been a constant reference for the medium’s application of the cinematographic illusion, especially once games became 3D. But none of this has barred the videogame event, its performance as an image, from its own creative evolution. For, the cinematographic illusion has still been made to serve and probed according to a new style. What could have once been described as a movement-image, has now been forced to change, fundamentally, in adopting the context of a videogame, notwithstanding remnant resemblances of the form that inspired it. A brief description of that cinematographic form will be necessary for what follows.
056
According to Deleuze (1983/2013, 1985/2003), cinema gives us real movement. That is, from the illusion of movement created by a series of still frames presented rapidly and at equal distance from each other, movement is ‘extracted from itself’ (Deleuze 1983/2013:24). From the illusion, cinema extracts ‘real movement’, not by adding movement to a still image, imitating natural perception, but via the mobilisation of a set. This mobilisation is accomplished through the movement of the camera and through cinematographic techniques like montage (Deleuze 1983/2013:25). The set of frames that originally formed a cinematographic illusion, is mobilised through a breach of the limitations of the subject. The camera moves freely and instantly through space and time and in this way generates real movement.
057
But it will not become clear exactly why or how this transcendence of subject and object constitutes real movement until the Bergsonian picture of movement Deleuze (1983/2013) had in mind is clarified. Real movement, for Bergson (1896/2004), is infinite in two complementary ways which nullify the movement from, and the distinction between, a subject and object.
058
Real movement is absolutely indivisible (Bergson 1896/2004:246-253, 1907/2007:194-200). When measuring a trajectory, we consider the changes in distance between two or more objects over time. The line imagined between the objects, which is divided into sections and used as a reference to describe the movement, does not convey the whole movement itself (Bergson 1896/2004:246). The line can be divided an infinite number of times, as shown in Zeno’s paradox (Aristotle 1999:143), but then we are only dividing ‘the line supposed to have been traversed, and not the movement traversing it’ (Bergson 1896/2004:246). The whole movement is already given instantly by our perception. Concrete parameters cannot be drawn around movement itself, only the divisible path we imagine to retrospectively describe it.
059
Real movement is absolutely relative (Bergson 1896/2004:254-255,267). In physics, movement can be considered a universal phenomenon of the acentred whole (Bergson 1896/2004:254). Everything is moving in relation to everything else on both a macro and a micro scale. Descriptions of these movements can only be extracted once a point of reference or centre is selected and a line is drawn between the points. But these details will only describe a change over time within those finite parameters. Real movement is relative to an infinite number of points in addition to our bodies. Thus, movements are acentred vibrations encompassing the entire universe.
060
Bergson (1896/2004) rejected distinctions between the subject and the object in the same way. The body is not immune to the above critique of parameters and limits; there is no absolute threshold separating it from other objects grouped together to understand the world; ‘the visual perception of an object, the brain, nerves, retina and the object itself form a connected whole’ (Bergson 1896/2004:285). Perception, as a movement, cannot be divided into sections and still retain the whole that is immediately given, as would be necessary to distinguish subject and object. Neither can one’s body be placed at the centre of all movements in the universe without losing the image of the whole movement (Bergson 1896/2004:260). In this way, pure subjectivity and individual identity are turned away from real movement.
061
Deleuze (1983/2013) used these same Bergsonian arguments against dualism to describe the cinema. In Bergson’s (1896/2004, 1907/2007) description of movement, there is always another division to be made, another body in space from which to measure the trajectory’s relative position and another position itself, constantly emerging. In cinema, Deleuze saw that there was always another shot, always another frame that is different from the one before it as an acentred movement through space and time. Thus, through montage, by virtue of the movement becoming acentred, cinema comes closer to real movement than natural perception. The essence of the cinema is to transcend the subject and the object through acentred movements.
062
At least, this is one way of describing the creative evolution that has taken place: the cinema’s gradual discovery of the mobilisation of its sections in pursuit of the new. This is what Deleuze (1983/2013:141-177; 1985/2003) charts in Cinema 1 and Cinema 2, from the centred action-image where everything is confined to react to the centre of a set, to the acentred time-image, where a linear narrative is abandoned to mobilise the image further. Cinema does not give us an illusion of movement through immobile sections, it mobilises sections through qualitative change or the production of the new (Deleuze 1983/2013:9).
063
Deleuze (1983/2013:24) asks, ‘What happened when the camera was fixed?’, leading to a description of ‘a primitive state of the cinema where the image is in movement rather than being a movement-image’. This is how the early cinema, its essence not yet realised, imitated the mediums that preceded it like theatre, photography or natural perception (Deleuze 1983/2013:25). The idea of montage had not yet emerged, so in this early version of the medium, ‘there is therefore no communication of mutually referring variable sets’ (Deleuze 1983/2013:24). Shots were stationary and the movements within were spatially determined (Deleuze 1983/2013:3,24). Movement was therefore localised to the things themselves. This is how, like scientific representations of movement, the cinematographic illusion amounts to points on a line over time (Deleuze 1983/2013:24). This absolute centre, around which the characters and objects are referenced, means relativity is also absent. While the movement-image’s genesis cannot be truly identified, for the same reason a point on a trajectory is only a convenience and can always be made more precise, we can at least say that early cinema did not yet provide what Deleuze calls a movement-image.
064
But this lack of mutually referring sets preventing early cinema from achieving a movement-image is what we find in most videogames, at least during gameplay. Montage generally goes against the idea of fluid and comprehensible gameplay. In the Final Fantasy VII Remake (Nomura et al. 2020) dance battle mentioned at 050, the player must push different buttons in time with the music to make Cloud dance. The camera is completely mobile, constantly cutting to different shots, making the entire process disorientating and difficult to play. I just wanted to watch Cloud dance but was instead forced to concentrate on a tiny spot on the screen telling me which button to push and when, as it changed position with every new shot.
(Nomura et al. 2020). This demonstrates a tension in videogame design between a desire to move towards what is appealing in a cinematographic language and the parameters necessary to make gameplay clear, intelligible, and fair for players.
065
Thus, videogames mark an involuntary return, in a sense, to the static camera of early cinema to clarify its gameplay elements. Restricting camera movement also restricts the key innovations of early cinema that led to the movement-image. There is, therefore, something fundamental in the gesture of videogames running counter to Bergson’s (1896/2004:337; 1907/2007) descriptions of real movement.
066
Again, cutscenes might be described as an exception when the camera ceases to provide a single continuous shot. But even if we follow Keogh’s (2018:118) argument and include cutscenes as part of the videogame event, cutscenes still depend on gameplay for the role they play in a videogame. As we saw at 050, cutscenes represent what is most analytically familiar to us and what is easiest to articulate in the phenomenon. However, this is not to suggest cutscenes and cinema are the same thing or have the same effect on the player. In the case of a cutscene, one’s orientation towards the image is completely different. Someone could walk in on the final cutscene of a Hideo Kojima (2008, 2015, 2019, etc.) game and feel like they were watching a movie, but the player would have a different experience. That difference emerges out of the cutscene’s proximity to gameplay for the player. During gameplay the camera is tied to a single point, like the playable character or arena, to facilitate player agency. That context precedes any Deleuzian images we could identify in the cutscene as a text. That history of agency frames what the cutscene means and what it is accomplishing. For, the production of a new videogame image is the result of player agency within the image-event. In contrast, the technique of montage is an interruption, a removal of agency. Compared to a videogame, a film has already happened. I had not finished taking in the futuristic depictions of Los Angeles in Blade Runner 2049 (Villeneuve 2017), but the story kept moving regardless. A videogame must give us time to act, in a way that is finite or in a way that is unrestricted like a novel or comics, if it is to be, as Galloway (2006:2) says, an ‘action’. And unlike other mediums, the videogame can also provide space, in addition to time, as another plane on which agency can be applied. One cost of this is the tethering of the camera, or point of view, to one’s decisions.
067
We can see this most clearly in 2D fighting games like Street Fighter (Funamizu et al. 1995; Nakayama 2016; Nishiyama 1987; Okamoto 1991; Okamoto et al. 1999; Tsukamoto 2008) where there are no changing perspectives, not even via the direction of a Lakitu Bro., and the camera is static, save some automatic adjustments along a two-dimensional plane to keep both players in view. Centred illusions of movement give us a single shot that is a side-on view of both players’ characters. Thus, while Deleuze (1983/2013) was able to redeem cinema from Bergson’s (1907/2007:194-195) critique of the cinematographic illusion through a description of cinema’s acentred movements, in Street Fighter the critique finds a new home. The character animations in Street Fighter, like early cinema, only imitate movement with a series of still frames and the by-product is an illusion and does not, according to Deleuze’s descriptions of cinema, constitute real movement.
(Funamizu et al. 1995).
068
Finishing moves are a brief exception to this in the franchise’s recent instalments. But these, as a rule, must be short because the player cannot control their character while they are happening. The insertion of cinematographic techniques here is used to communicate the strategic value of the move and elevate the spectacle and drama of a fight. But there is a feeling that the game proper has been paused in these instances, like watching a replay in sport after missing the actual in-game occurrence it is a reproduction of;
(Nakayama 2016). Once this brief change in perspective has been accomplished, we can get back to the fight, along with all the rigid parameters that make it possible.
069
We can see in Street Fighter that this apprehension of the camera does place a kind of limit on space and time as a result of having given them to the player. In cinema, the viewer has no control over these ‘forms of intuition’ (Kant 1781/2007:B305) presented (performed) in the image, but the camera relishes in their manipulation. Street Fighter only exists within a set of tight parameters, an idea obviously counter to Bergson’s (1907/2007) descriptions of real movement. The actions available to the player are limited; there are three punch buttons, three kick buttons and a directional pad or stick to move the player’s avatar left and right, to crouch and jump. A border encloses the animations, and nothing exists outside it. The available illusions of movement create a finite illusion of space. After a match, the illusion of space and time is destroyed, for, available contributing actions are zero and the successive equidistant frames have ceased. In this way, the parameters of Street Fighter, as a videogame ‘circumscribed within limits of space and time’ (Caillois 1961/2001:9), come from the limits placed on the illusions of movement in-game. The animations or illusions of movement as actions chosen by the player, not movement-images, are foundational to the entire experience of Street Fighter. The player has extended a part of themselves into the event of the image or cinematographic illusion as it is being produced and some limitations are necessary for that to occur; developers give players a finite set of possibilities.
070
This is how the human participates in the production of a videogame image, i.e. the event of image production in dialogic collaboration with a developer who has established parameters to create a space for player agency. This problematic emerged for us, and we are so far coming to terms with it, in the same way we came to terms with the fixed camera following Mario in Super Mario 64. That is, via the concepts of, and in contrast to, the cinema.
071
The description of videogames in this way, as a human event evolving with and against other human events, namely cinema, is counter to agendas that seek to define what a videogame is. Rough (2018:33), in his definition, gives some examples of videogames without any visual component at all, like ‘videogames for the visually impaired’, to prove that video or images are non-essential to the phenomenon. But the character animations are an essential part of Street Fighter and autonomy over them is how we experience the game. This is not to use Street Fighter to say something general that can be applied to all videogames. Rather, Street Fighter reveals something the videogame is doing with the cinematographic illusion. This speaks to the possibilities of human experience in relation to videogames as a dialogic and evolving event. As an event, rigid definitions of the videogame prevent us from following along with it.
Though I persistently combine the words ‘video’ and ‘game’ to encourage an understanding of the videogame as its own seperate phenomenon and not a combination of the two, in the situation of the present paragraph we could say ‘video-game’ in the same way Wittgenstein (1953/2009:22) employs the term, ‘language-game’. Like language, videogames are not something to be confined with criteria but are a means by which we generate new criteria and associations. This, along with other developments yet to be fully unfolded, makes me wish for a new term, but I am constrained by a desire to not immediatly alienate my partners in the performance of this image, so ‘videogame’ is my compromise.
 We will use the word ‘game’ to refer to a videogame for the same reason. ‘Game’, ‘gaming’ and ‘gamer’ are all words used to identify with and refer to the experience of playing a videogame. Why should we not allow the phenomenon of the videogame to change and influence our understanding of what a game is — discover new uses for it in the ‘language-game’ (Wittgenstein 1953/2009:22)? Games are also human events, as are video and the cinematographic illusion. Videogames allow us to explore the coalescence of these elements within — not as elements of — an entirely new mode. If we set criteria prior to any engagement with the event itself, we risk occluding our view of the emergence of the new.
I risk concealing from myself the performation of this image.
072
At 066 I mentioned the possibility of regarding a typical Kojima (2008, 2015, 2019, etc.) game as a film and it is possible to do this with any videogame. Videogames have the potential to contain films within them – a movie could be playing on a TV screen in the gameworld, a cutscene can be taken out of context, player autonomy can be ignored and we can pretend that gameplay is just another shot of a film. Some have taken this to mean the videogame, and those ambitions attached to it like VR, represent the next evolutionary step in visual media through a lens of progressivism. Our own Kojima (cited in Gibson 2006) famously gave license to this line of thought when he said,
…Art is the stuff you find in the museum, whether it be a painting or a statue. What I’m doing, what videogame creators are doing, is running the museum - how do we light up things, where do we place things, how do we sell tickets? For better or worse, what I do, Hideo Kojima, myself, is run the museum and also create the art that’s displayed in the museum.
But here, Kojima is describing an inherent quality of images generally. Everyone who sees the painting, Whistler’s Mother (Whistler 1871), notices the painting within a painting, hanging on the wall. It has always been possible to contain images within other images, leading to new contexts and experiences in relation to the image. While videogames certainly entail new ways of encountering images, we must not forget that Kojima’s museum is, itself, an image. This is not a feature that makes videogames a unique human experience, neither is it why they are interesting. Even generous definitions like Kojima’s are unhelpful here.
073
We can make a critique of Kojima’s definition of the videogame which resembles Žižek’s (2008:200) description of cyberspace, regarding the ‘unbearable closure of being’. Unlike an actual museum, the gameworld is inevitably closed, there is a finitude in how the experience is contained and does not overflow us. Of course, a videogame can be infinite in a mathematical sense, in a sense convenient to the computer. But Žižek’s point is that this type of infinity is exactly what leads to the closure, a phenomenon revealing all its parts indefinitely with nothing that is, in principle, inaccessible. A randomly generated world might extend forever while at the same time concealing very little. In videogames, while we regard them in this way, we do not need to wonder; we do not need an excess.
074
Now, if there is something that distinguishes the various image mediums like painting, photography, cinema or videogames, it is not that each one represents a higher order, like the idea that each new medium contains all the previous ones within it. It is that each medium entails a new way of using images to orientate us towards the real, towards something irreducible and open. In the case of videogames specifically it is towards the image itself as immanent lived experience, towards the image as an irreducible event. Hence, this project is interested in videogames for the way they orient us in relation to the event of the image, which is to move beyond the perception of the videogame image being a collection of calculated and pre-planned frames, and beyond the videogame image as a limited version of Deleuze’s (1983/2013) movement-image.
075
Compared to other mediums today, paintings provide a profound lack of stimulation and detail regarding space and time; we have to lend those elements to it ourselves. There is not enough detail to make out most of the dancers in Renoir’s (1876) Bal du moulin de la Galette, not to mention all those that have been chopped off in the framing of the image. But ‘chopped off’ is a photographic way of describing it anyway, inappropriate for a painting. The dancers who are not in the frame, those which Renoir never painted are an essential component of the experience of the image and, in one important sense, the whole point. We are not referring to the closure we see in Gestalt theory, where the incomplete fragments of an object suggest the whole to perception. Rather, the entire purpose of that which was painted is to persuade us towards a more vague and mysterious experience of that which was not. Becoming acquainted with the latter, which is to make our contribution to the painting as viewer, is what pulls us into the picture. If every border, the moment we experience that boundary, spawned a new image, we will have indeed stumbled into Žižek’s (2008) ‘unbearable closure’. The confinement of the painting directs us towards the mystery of the foreclosure of the lived event of the image as something which cannot be fully described.
076
Cinema can be described in a mode like this too, not as a moving painting, but as an alternate performance which is an orienting ourselves, our bodies, in relation to the real. Cinema not only enables movement through space within the image itself, but the montage allows images to be juxtaposed and communicate temporally. The role of time here is an important thing to note because comics, McCloud (1994:7-8) famously illustrates, also juxtapose images sequentially, albeit through space on the page. Next to comics, cinema is the creative evolution of sequential images when the temporal experience of each section has been surrendered over to the machine, or to the film-maker via the machine. This process, along with photography and the cinematographic illusion, enabled the image to become something new, as we have seen Deleuze (1983/2013; 1985/2003) explore. Because we have no control over the camera, a sense of wonder can be constantly evoked through the composition of the shot and montage. The cinema is, in a sense, always beyond us and outside our control and this constitutes the image-event of cinema.
077
And this is all another way of describing what Antoine de Saint-Exupéry (1943/2020) was expressing when he wrote The Little Prince. At the end of the story, the Little Prince is dying and the star that is his home is too small to point out; ‘It’s better that way. My star will be just another star in the sky. That way you’ll love watching all the stars’ (Saint-Exupéry 1943/2020:100). The Little Prince understands the need for mystery to give things meaning, to be able to give ourselves to them, to surrender.
078
Are videogames, then, another example of the image’s yielding to closure, like Žižek’s (2008) descriptions of cyberspace? Star Citizen (Roberts n.d.), No Man Sky (Murry et al. 2016) or Elite Dangerous (Braben 2014) represent an ambition in videogames that nullifies the Little Prince’s dying gesture. They are moving towards a gameworld in which the stars and planets players see in the sky are accessible destinations, which can be intimately explored. The orbiting planets in Outer Wilds (Beachum 2019) are something to be accessed, solved and overcome, rather than something to be overcome by.
079
This brings us to a more revealing problematic to be explored in relation to videogames than any attempt at definitions. That is to identify the medium’s point of openness, the location of the event. Where is the irreducible event in the videogame as an image? And this is the same as asking where the human is in the videogame image. This can be answered with a description of how the image is made, or how we collaborate in the production of a videogame image in a way that is unambiguous, on display, part of the image itself, not an interpretation – the image becomes an explicit human event.
080
At 063 we located illusions of movement in the cinema’s genesis but the same cannot be said of the videogame’s origins because, as I keep saying, the videogame constitutes an entirely different human event, despite its appropriation of cinematographic gestures and notwithstanding my use of cinema to leverage this analysis of the videogame image. In cinema, the illusion was something to grow out of, it had to be transgressed for the sake of its creative evolution. In contrast to this, the early forms of interactive media were text-based, and the cinematographic illusion was later adopted as a supplemental — a spectacle that the industry immediately identified as its quest. Thus, videogames have never needed to mobilise the image, from the beginning the illusion was immediately incorporated as one of many mobile interactive elements.
081
In Street Fighter and other 2D fighting games, we will see that the images are mobile, constituting ‘real movement’ in the Bergsonian sense, even when the task of playing the game requires one to understand them as illusions. Though the game’s mechanics are built around the cinematographic illusion, we will identify indivisible movement in the broad interaction that is required to learn and play the game as a dialogic human event. Further, the more in-game animations are considered to be illusions of movement, the more meaning they have in the context of the game and are mobilised as constituents of the event. In Street Fighter, illusions of movement can break down and become real events along a different trajectory, namely player agency and dialogue. This case shows how the effort to think with and along illusions of movement in the context of a videogame image does not compromise the legitimacy of the performance which constitutes a human event. To clarify how this works in Street Fighter, we will need to explore the game’s basic and complex mechanics and what they demand from the player.
082
Playing Street Fighter well can entail a deep understanding of its character animations. This is true to the point where the illusions of movement are broken, and animations are temporarily regarded to be a series of separate equidistant still images. Each character animation has a set number of startup, active and recovery frames. Startup frames describe how many frames of animation take place before any damage is inflicted on the opponent, during which the player is vulnerable. Active frames are those in which an attack does damage if the opponent crosses the path of the action and fails to block. Recovery frames are those frames in which a character is completing the animation and cannot yet commence a new one, during which the player is vulnerable. This ‘frame-data’ can be central to how we play the game, especially at a high level. A familiarity is recommended, at least with the character one plays the most (Munday 2016:257). For example, Sakura’s standing light punch in Street Fighter V (Nakayama 2016) has 4 frames on startup, 3 active and 8 recovery compared to her standing heavy punch which has 9 frames on startup, 3 active and 21 recovery (Capcom n.d.):
(Nakayama 2016). The latter does more damage but requires more startup and recovery frames and is therefore less safe. This vulnerability can be overcome, however, by using combos, i.e. linking certain attacks together so that the proceeding attack connects to or interrupts the recovery frames of the attack before it. Frame-data also plays a role in ‘frame trapping’ which is to create a situation where an opponent’s attack can be interrupted with a counter-attack that has fewer startup frames (Munday 2016:260). Though it is not essential, familiarity with frame-data can make frame trapping possible in many situations. Thus, character animations in Street Fighter come to have meaning beyond the illusions of movement. In many ways, understanding the animations as illusions is important to comprehend the mechanics of the game.
083
Note that an appreciation of frame-data does not supplant the spectacle of the cinematographic illusion, but can become part of our lived experience with/in/of the image-event. Attempting to combo into a special attack means trying to complete the action within a set number of frames but an awareness of these frames does not diminish the effects of the illusions of movement on perception. Our chances of winning improve as the concept of individual frames comes to determine our actions. The frames constitute the data of a science of Street Fighter; we use frame-data to articulate and think through potential and occurring scenarios, to solve the problems emerging from a lived image-event. Our skills improve in Street Fighter as an awareness of perceived movement as illusion increasingly comes to determine how we play, our decisions and how we, as irreducible beings, live an irreducible event. This learned new orientation towards the image, despite its disassembling the image into separate frames, and despite its abstracting thought away from the perceptions of the body — more than ‘despite’ but through this very process — the image comes to have greater meaning in the immediate lived experience of the player. Players do not count frames in real-time during a match but character animations are manipulated as illusions of movement. The more we play Street Fighter the clearer these features of the image-event become, a different posture is assumed and we are more practised at letting these hidden elements of the image influence our performance. Frame-data plays an important role in understanding the advantages and disadvantages of one’s chosen character in certain situations. The game’s developer, Capcom (n.d.), makes all of Street Fighter V’s (Nakayama 2016) frame-data available on their website and certain tools in that game’s training mode are designed to facilitate a comprehension of frame-data while practising. Learning to play Street Fighter can now be described as the process of discovering more dialogic aspects within the image-event, but this discovery must be immediately lived, not intellectually or philosophically realised, the experience of the image is not deconstructed or hampered in any way, to play Street Fighter skilfully.
084
This means frame-data, as a concept, is not essential to play or even win the game, but anyone’s actions within the image will, inevitably and over time, come to account for these symptoms of the image; the player must still navigate these facts of the videogame (Munday 2016:256-257). Playing Street Fighter conditions actions to be partially determined by the fact that the animations in the game are not ‘real movements’. For example, it is possible to learn through trial and error that special attacks can interrupt recovery frames. This phenomenon is only possible because the animations are finite and divisible. Exploiting this fact does not entail cheating or breaking the game but the exact opposite, it is how we succeed in the 2D fighting game genre, it is how these games are meticulously designed to be played and discovered. So, concerning what is perceived as movement in-game, to win at Street Fighter one needs to unlearn what movement is, according to natural intuition, to perform the movement differently, in unnatural ways. This unlearning/unseeing will be comprehended not only intellectually but through the body, through muscle memory which superposes a formal knowledge of frame-data.
085
What is perceived as movement in Street Fighter is a finite series of still images and comprehending this illusion is a process that resembles scientific inquiry. Outside videogames, to modify real movement or intervene in an action from one’s own body or that of another, we already divide movement artificially (Bergson 1907/1907:191-192). This process of division can go on forever indefinitely in scientific inquiry, never getting any closer to real movement (Bergson 1907/2007:214-215). However, the illusions of movement in Street Fighter are phenomena scientific thought can get to the bottom of, and getting to the ‘bottom’ of game animations will increase the influence we have, as players striving for a win-state, over the videogame image. The animations are divided into sections to intervene in an action, but unlike real movement, this process has an end. The ceiling is rapidly reached, and no further divisions are necessary, the inquiry is complete. A clear definite division separating cause and effect can be easily located. Thus, the future can be grasped in its entirety, the production of the new, its ever upspringing, can be stayed and we can win the game.
086
This is what happens in a combo string. Because the movements are not real and the number of frames is finite, the effect of a cause can be locked in until the string of connected attacks is over. There is no frame gap in which the opponent can intervene a counter and the number of possible actions their avatar can perform has been reduced to zero. At the start of a match, possible illusions of movement orbit each player’s character in a virtual plane.
By ‘virtual’ I do not mean that which is computated but the whole from which potentialities are actualised (see Deleuze 1998/2018:60).
 There is potentiality surrounding the character, multiple pathways present themselves before they are forced to coalesce into a single point, a single animation or illusion of movement. A match in Street Fighter can thus be described as a series of fluctuations, with the number of potential actions expanding and shrinking. Each player is attempting to force the virtual lines into a single point which is a win-state. This is accomplished through a performance of the image which accounts for the movement’s nature, as illusions, as was described above in the form of frame-data, to enable frame trapping and strategic combo strings.
087
From this view, in Street Fighter, autonomy is what generates real, irreducible, movement; agency mobilises still frames through creative unforeseeable gestures. Finite parameters constitute the strategic elements of the game and the deeper the grasp one has of these, the more meaningful, intentional and autonomous one’s actions in the game become. The creative comprehension and manipulation of finite significations is an indivisible movement towards a win-state.
088
This indivisible path or movement could so far be described as centred, framed via a description of how to win the game, or a single player’s experience with a computer, pushing towards a singular outcome. But the actual event of the videogame image is an acentred movement because multiple agencies are working in a dialogic situation. These forces are in productive conflict in any videogame instance, even in single-player games. It remains for us to examine the constituents of a Street Fighter match as a shared becoming, a single creative event.
089
To play Street Fighter is to participate in an inter-human dialogue, which is both visual and kinaesthetic. During a match, the actions of the opponent, displayed on the monitor, can be regarded as a series of questions which demand a response. These should not be thought of as literal questions, only as impetuses which are directed at the player and which demand action. These questions would lose all meaning if the player was absent, they are what it means to play the game and participate in Street Fighter as an act of dialogue. Responses are, in turn, also questions to an opponent and what is shared in this mutuality constitutes the novel dialogue of the game. This is only made possible through the unique visual language of illusions of movement provided by the game’s developers via the apparatus.
090
This dialogue is irreducible, however, and the separate elements of question and answer can mislead us from this fact. The question precedes the response in time, it anticipates a response, but the question has not become fully actualised, its full meaning and implications do not emerge, until after the answer has been given. The question to which the action was a reply becomes suddenly more apparent, it becomes Real. But an answer is also a question and that question will be constantly renewed, taking on new meaning and becoming mobilised in the unfolding events of a match. This new meaning cannot be predicted just as an opponent’s emerging response will continue to introduce new and unforeseen elements. The dialogue itself is not located in the back-and-forth motion of question and answer. It happens in neither the question nor the answer, not in one player or the other or the developer’s anticipations. It is, following Buber (1947/2004; 1965) here, in the in-betweenness of the participants.
091
We can come closer to understanding the shared creative act of a Street Fighter match by furthering this resemblance to Buberian dialogue and borrowing some distinctions Buber (1947/2004:10-12) made, namely ‘observing’, ‘looking on’ and ‘becoming aware’.
We should not employ these terms as a means of distinguishing different phenomena because every videogame image (and every image generally) can be regarded in a way that is dialogic. Rather, the terms should be regarded as a means of distinguishing the different ways we orient ourselves towards the phenomena and not as a description of the phenomena itself. The examples I use below belie false distinctions within the phenomena itself, but they do represent instances where the orientation in question comes more easily, so let them stand.
092
We could say we are ‘observing’ when we play against a dummy in the practice mode of Street Fighter V (Nakayama 2016) which has been programmed to perform a series of moves in a desired sequence. But dialogue will never emerge from our interaction with this dummy, its animations will be learnt in a rote manner and only ‘observed’ (Buber 1947/2004:10). That is, we can accumulate recognisable traits and patterns that can be memorised and this can play an important part of what is required to improve one’s skills and anticipate the sequence of moves we have programmed, but this only points towards and cannot replace the dialogic act of playing a match where indetermination extends beyond us into an otherness.
093
A deeper understanding can come from describing what Buber (1947/2004:10) calls ‘the onlooker’. Here, we disregard any attempt to learn individual elements, remember or even acknowledge them. The phenomenon is taken as a whole, as a single impression. We say to ourselves, ‘I will not try to “figure out” my opponent in a deliberate and calculated way, I will seize the advantages that come from skipping all that, looking passed separate elements or instances at something more complete’. This gesture, like observing, plays an important role in the process of learning the game and winning a match. There is a general awareness that only comes from prolonged experience with the game, which also applies to the non-specific comprehension of an opponent. While this can bring us closer to the human or object that is our subject in many ways, we have not yet arrived at the kind of awareness and state that is only made possible through dialogue.
094
This ‘becoming aware’ (Buber 1947/2004:12) is identifiable in Street Fighter because the actions of my opponent in the game, the questions described above, are all to a subject, that is, me or you. What is being displayed on the monitor ‘speaks something that enters into [our] own life’ and demands our immediate action (Buber 1947/2004:11). In the previous two kinds of awareness, the human or object we are seeking to comprehend is separate from us but in Street Fighter ‘this man is not my object; I have got to do with him’ (Buber 1947/2004:11). A film or book can say things that feel unique and personal based on how we interpret a text, but these are subjective; what is unique in videogames, the novelty that is emerging, is shared between us in that moment and is incomplete. This is a novelty that we become aware of because we are part of its emergence.
095
In Street Fighter, the fact that there are win and lose states distracts us from the dialogic nature of the phenomenon, but the competitiveness of the game does not inhibit dialogue. Competition entails more than setting one’s opponent at a distance from oneself to establish difference — I won, you lost. For Elias (1978:130), player interdependence can entail both ‘an interdependence of allies or of opponents’ and our use of Buber (1965:69) here paints the latter as acceptance of otherness:
…man learns not merely that he is limited by man, cast upon his own finitude, partialness, need of completion, but his own relation to truth is heightened by the other’s different relation to the same truth.
In a Street Fighter match, we are confronted with the paradox of an alternative interpretation of the same truths, the same finite game mechanics, which are yet not the same and cannot be controlled or predicted. This phenomenon amounts to something of a violence on our person, on that distance presupposed, an attack on the objectification of the other,
…each becomes aware of the other and is thus related to him in such a way that he does not regard and use him as an object, but as his partner in a living event, even if it is no more than a boxing match. (Buber 1965:64)
A distance is present before a match begins, and a distance is established by the outcome of the match, but these facts should only throw into relief for us what is mutual during the match itself.
096
The spectacle of violence can distract from the dialogic event we have just described. Consider a more extreme example like Mortal Kombat (Boon 2002, 2004, 2006, 2008, 2011, 2015, 2019; Boon and Tobias 1992, 1993, 1995, 1997), a series of two-dimensional fighting games famous for their gruesome depictions of violence. In Mortal Kombat 11 (Boon 2019), Cassie Cage’s default fatality entails kicking her opponent in the chest so hard it punctures their body, sending their heart across the arena.
(Boon 2019). In Mortal Kombat, the opponent’s avatar becomes a piece of meat that is played with for laughs in a variety of ways to celebrate victory. It must be acknowledged that this spectacle is completely at odds with the entering into relation that dialogue entails. What once represented an opponent has become a dead object to be controlled. There is no mutual confirmation of alternative truths to be reconciled. Here, the violent image ignores the contradiction of the other. But these images are not what will inevitably come to determine one’s actions in a match. We can conceive of instances when a particular move is performed for the sake of how it demonstrates dominance over an opponent, but Mortal Kombat 11 (Boon 2019) requires a strategic application of the illusions of movement in the same way Street Fighter does. These rapidly come to determine our actions more than the visual appeal of the perceived movements themselves, realistic graphics or violence. The last of these stands out, because of how violence seems most clearly at odds with what is generally perceived as productive dialogue. But illusions of movement in general, or any gesture towards realism can also be construed as a type of violence against the irreducible because everything becomes its object. In contrast, dialogue, as Buber (1965) presents it, describes a posture in which otherness contributes to an event, which we are also a part of, but our intentions are directed towards the event itself and not its otherness.
097
This description of Street Fighter as dialogue, can now serve our framing of videogames around the Bergsonian philosophy of the new. There are unprecedented game-states that the developers could not have anticipated — they can only create fences. Though the computer contains, in its programming, a finite list of possible actions, it cannot predict what our next action will be. It can only take a catalogue of all previous actions and become along with us. In videogames we participate in a new instance of movement, real novelty because to play a videogame is to be more than a consumer, it is to participate in a mutual becoming, which works to produce the image.
098
Humans participate together to form a videogame image in a shared movement and this does not exclude the apparatus. One can have a dialogic encounter with a piece of nature or art or whenever we become with an object, where it ceases to be an object for us, and we slide together into an in-betweenness. This phenomenon, in the case of videogames, can be observed in a very literal sense. There is a shared dependency between the human and the apparatus to perform a mutual transformation. The thing that is evolving resides in neither party alone but is a literal coming together, a single movement like Bergson’s (1896/2004:285) description of perception. When we play Street Fighter and are not pulling ourselves out of the act of play through deliberate objectification of the game or an opponent, the schism separating subject and object is abandoned as non-essential to the image-event and is a philosophical distraction from our performance of the image as real event. This is the case even while playing alone against the computer. Our movements become the illusions of ‘real movement’ on the screen, for there is no definite threshold separating ourselves and all other elements we could identify as contributing to the videogame image.
099
In multiplayer instances, a creative event shared between a player, developers and hardware is what enters into relation with another human opponent to form another image, another possible framing of an irreducible event. In this instance, it is even harder to discern or justify a dialogic centre and in one sense the event becomes more real or decisions have more interpersonal weight. Playing Street Fighter against another person is, for most people, always preferable. There is a realness to the movements, even while playing against a stranger online, that is absent against the computer or against what the game’s development team have anticipated. We could describe multiplayer as a dialogue between two players that is mediated by a machine and developers, like two dialogues entering into a third dialogue. But this suggests that multiple creative acts are happening simultaneously, which is misleading. We can artificially distinguish separate elements, separate dialogic gestures, but the shared becoming itself should ultimately be regarded as a single human event – where parameters lose meaning.
100
This shared becoming between humans and machines is further complicated when we consider a temporally offset dialogue happening between players and game developers. With a computer, the developer lays out a finite plane predicting and responding to every possible action from the player, enabling and restricting, expanding and shrinking possible actions. To then play the game entails cutting a path through that finite plane, replacing the possible with the potential, the finite with the virtual. We are never playing a videogame by ourselves because there is a constant and indirect collaboration with the developers to create the emerging image-event. What makes this exchange irreducible and dialogic is that the path itself cannot be foreseen because of player and developer agency – except a temporal dissonance makes this mutuality uneven.
This uneven power distribution is resolved in the lived event of playing a videogame, if we regard it as being ‘determined-in-the-last-instance’ (see Laruelle 1996/2013:125).
 The content of the plane, prior to the game’s development, cannot be predicted because developing a game is its own creative act. However, the game’s gesture is incomplete until it is actioned by a player and when a player performs an action the developer’s response has literally already taken place. Player and developer mutually depend on each other in a shared production of the new.
101
This final dialogue between developers and players serves to show how many different elements are being brought into relation in the event of a Street Fighter match. We can artificially isolate separate dialogic instances of mutual becoming, one between the human and the apparatus, another between two humans in the moment of a match and another between the player and developer. These disparate participants should be regarded as converging elements that form a single creative and social event. While Goffman (1963:17) extended the scope of social situations to encompass ‘physical copresence, tele-phonic connection or letter exchange’, there is both an immediacy and potentiality to videogames which makes them unique. The player enters into a becoming where agency is central, but there is also a kind of submission to larger pre-planned transformations. The emergence taking place is based on a mutual inheritance which has already partially happened and yet remains unresolved.
102
Notwithstanding the technical feat involved in the broad phenomenon that is Street Fighter and the extent to which it has been created in this respect, by virtue of the coalescence of multiple dialogic elements, it remains fundamentally a becoming that is facilitated by technology. Like Handelman’s (2005) picture of ritual, Street Fighter and other videogames can be assigned their own autonomy and are thus independent happenings. This has a tendency of being masked when we are distracted by the spectacle of the apparatus itself in the quantitative pursuit of realism or violence. As we have seen, identifying the videogame too closely with other mediums like cinema is a potential source of misdirection, away from the irreducible human event. This can also be true of animation.
103
I have used the word ‘animation’ above, along the way of describing the dialogic event which produces a videogame image with specific reference to Street Fighter. Animation can also be regarded as a separate medium, distinct from both cinema and videogames, but here we will regard it as a means or method which can be employed in cinema or videogames in the process of producing an image. Other image methods could include the camera, the gamepad controlling the camera, the game engine, etc. — none of which replace animation but together with animation codetermine the image. Animation is a means of producing an image by generating frames. Videogame players work with developers and artists, in a small capacity in terms of labour but in a large capacity in terms of the decisive last instant which chooses the next frame, to produce an animated image.
104

105
The animation can lend itself to the videogame event, to convey the image as an event, but it must just as frequently yield to gameplay. This submission has been described as a point of good game design. Animation is the crux where developers communicate a game’s most important information, the possibilities of the unfolding image, to the player. They are also an essential element of fighting game animation because they are necessary for communication, for the developer to communicate the possibilities of the image to the player, for the player to communicate and accommodate for their opponent’s recognition of their intentions, the broadcasting of their choices within the image itself.
106
Videogames have discovered and developed a particular animation style that fulfils the needs of dialogue. For example, in Rayman 2 (Ancel 1999) we can see traces of the feeling of gameplay in the animations of Rayman, the playable character. Rayman feels fast and snappy because the player can initiate the jump at any moment: from the middle of a roll, hanging on a ledge or the moment Rayman’s feet touch the ground, we can jump again.

107
Animation, in and of itself, does not enter Deleuze’s (1983/2013, 1985/2003) concepts of the cinema.
I first read Deleuze’s (1983/2013, 1985/2003) cinema books while I was learning how to play Street Fighter V (Nakayama 2016). I took notes in the margins and underlined passages which seemed to conflict with, or reveal certain aspects of, the phenomenon of the fighting game I had immersed myself in. I can recall one, perhaps imagined case of Deleuze mentioning animation in that read-through but have since been unable to locate it. ‘Animation’ does not appear in the index of Cinema 1 or Cinema 2. I opened a PDF of each book — the object on my shelf has become an event on my monitor full of potentialities. I perform an OCR (optical character recognition) scan using Adobe Acrobat Pro DC (Adobe 1993) on the PDF document to convert it into searchable text, press Crtl + F, type ‘animation’ and press enter. Between the two books there is only one instance of that word occurring. It is possible the OCR failed to recognise particular instances like, for example, if the word ‘animation’ were spread across two lines with a hyphen. That single occurrence of ‘animation’ is in an endnote on page 230 in reference to Vertov (cited in Deleuze 1983/2013:230). This is the bases of my conclusion that Deleuze has no interest in animation and my reading of the text with a mind preoccupied with frame-data and character animations was not to extract a theory of animation that can be applied to the cinema; I was thinking about videogames via the cinema in a way that is similar to how this page commenced, leveraging the theoretical and conceptual maturity of ideas about an entirely different medium to stimulate original thought by challenging them in a new mode foreign to them.
 We can take an animated film and easily identify within it whatever varieties of movement-image we can discern, ignoring the fact that these images have been accomplished through animation. What animation does for the cinema takes place along a different line of thought. Brad Bird (2018) was asked in an interview to comment on the recent Disney adaptations (Branagh 2015; Favreau 2016, 2019; Ritchie 2019) of their classic animated films like Cinderella (Geronimior et al. 1950), The Jungle Book (Reitherman 1967) The Lion King (Allers and Minkoff 1994) or Aladdin (Musker and Clements 1992) into live action: What is being accomplished in this retreat from animation in pursuit of realism? Bird’s response came to the defence of animation as ‘its own thing’ against images of movement captured through a camera. Photorealism, ‘…is not the reason to do animation anyway, the reason to do animation is caricature’. In these terms, animation is more than the reduction of an image into its essence, it is the enhancement of those faculties which make it distinct, specifically pertaining to movement. This caricature of movement can be implemented and lend itself to the cinema or the videogame but in each case, it is fulfilling a different need, contributing to a different event. Animation serves the cinema in reference to narrative and character, and though these are still relevant factors in how a game tells its story, animation in the videogame also must communicate to everyone involved in the event the possibilities and outcomes of the image as a dialogic event.
108
One of my first naive goals for this project, in the early stages of its conceptualisation, was to describe real movement in Street Fighter à la the movement-image Deleuze (1983/2013, 1985/2003) identified in cinema. The failure of this intention is what bore fruit, revealing how radically different the videogame is from the cinema. For it seemed that, according to Deleuze’s (1983/2013:24) analysis of primitive cinema, Street Fighter ought to entail the cinematographic illusion par excellence. But Street Fighter, and videogames more generally, need to be regarded as an entirely separate phenomenon with a radically different essence so as to make this connection invalid. If we take player agency to be the fundamental gesture of interactive media (developers are players too), the image is immediately mobilised according to the same Bergsonian definitions of movement Deleuze employed, albeit in a completely different way.
109
If videogames are regarded as events with their own evolving autonomy, we must acknowledge that they are unique and unprecedented in this regard. Their finite elements often include the generation and navigation of a spatial plane along a set of predetermined parameters, but in Street Fighter we have shown that these parameters can harbour infinite significance when their conception becomes the object of human striving and when they are integrated into the irreducible gestures of dialogue. The multifaceted nature of the event also makes it unique; videogames entail a creative evolution that is shared between disparate parties including the human, the non-human and the temporally displaced human. These coalesce in a single unpredictable moment that is its own autonomous event. Deleuze (1985/2003:280) regarded filmmakers as philosophers, he did philosophy with the cinema, but videogames need to happen to anthropology (012). That is, they need to emerge for us as human events which take place within the process of producing an image. Videogames are presently interhuman and in them we can see the emerging human; a videogame is produced as an event, not an object.
110
We have previously framed the videogame image as an object to be understood because we started on familiar ground, namely the cinema. Not only that, we began from a place that could motivate this project, so that the videogame was transcendent (not transcendental), external, Other, an object to ask questions about and to be understood. The videogame’s difference in relation to the cinema, its lack of a Deleuzian movement-image, was leveraged to discover the videogame image as an open dialogic event. But we cannot separate from this discovery an implicit challenge to our original question, which posed the videogame image as an object. We can preserve the image-object by simply pushing it outside the image-event; the image is a by-product of what has been described. Then our descriptions of the videogame as an interhuman, complex social event on its own terms, specifically according to how we play Street Fighter, pose no threat to the integrity of the unaddressed image which perhaps cannot be described or does not need to be described because it is its own description of itself. But of course, both these can be true for anything and, rather, they describe postures we assume more than any thing we are trying to understand. This is our current posture: in a competitive Street Fighter match, we try to force the image to actualise in a certain way, and so does our opponent and so are/were all the developers. Our collective actions are pointed towards the resolution of this indeterminate object: the image. This took some work, the paradigm meets resistance, because software, as a general phenomenon, as a commodity, hides even the event of its production, framing itself only as an object which is consumed or ‘played’ in a non-complex and uni-directional manner. Let the dialogic event of image production, as it has been revealed above, now stand. It remains for us to accept the videogame’s invitation to assume a posture for which there is no image-object, which posture is the discovery of the transcendental image. So rather than acting on the videogame object for the purposes of analysis, our new goal is to experience the videogame event acting on us. This means recognising in the above initial analysis an invitation to assume a new posture, which demands we forsake our goals towards the full elucidation of the videogame image, discrete and novel in relation to other mediums, and let the videogame change how we think about all mediums. So the task becomes for us to better come to terms with this posture, to recognise that we are part of the image itself, to further frame the videogame image as transcendental.

Software and the problem with digital objects (a)

111
The term or concept ‘computer graphics’ is a symptom of a mode of thought, an intellectual posture which is a new location for the image-object after the discovery of the image-event, meaning the above discovery of the dialogic event in Street Fighter as it pertains to the videogame phenomenon and ultimately images generally. The persistence of ‘computer graphics’ or ‘computer-generated images’ is how the dialogic event can still entail an orientation where the image is something we consume or produce, where the image appears as the by-product of an event. So far, the transcendental image has been mentioned but not yet discovered for the sake of the big way of performing Videogames for a New Anthropological Image. Our path to this will be a new problematic with computer graphics in mind, where the image’s primary source, the device, is yet to be fully explored. Below we will see that regardless of how said device produces the image to be consumed (it could be rasterized or ray-traced on a CRT, LCD, OLED, etc.) the event of these technologies constitutes an instance of the human body participating in a complex dialogic event. Beyond users as consumers and devices as producers, the user’s role as producer de jure, and their merger with the device itself, will be revealed. This can be called our discovery of a more basic dialogue unfolding between the user and device, where the device (a monitor, television, smartphone, Gameboy Advance, Neo Geo Pocket, etc.), accounts for a more active and complex element of the dialogic event this page has so far been describing. The upshot here will be a further receding of the image-object to finally reveal a way of regarding the videogame image as transcendental. To honour what we have been developing and our burgeoning new understanding of the videogame, we will not regard a device to be the sole giver of digital images by not referring to the images in question as ‘computer-generated images’. The body and the screen are mere constituents within an image-event which will now be further brought into focus.
112
We have already seen that, ultimately, the device’s function in an image-event is not simply to give our perceptual organs an image to receive. The experience of the digital image cannot be separated from other, more fundamental aspects of the event like user and developer intentionalities. This already means that the images we feel we ‘receive’ from our displays hold a special quality. Before we even think of them as images, they are distinct from the objects we say they represent. This is to say, they are specially perceived or set apart from other objects in the world in the act of perception itself prior to thought. This aligns with a more basic intuition; of course, we are never fooled by the image into thinking it otherwise, into thinking we are actually looking at the thing-that-an-image-is-of, and neither are our bodies. This is because viewing a digital image, the simple act of looking presupposes a specific set of perceptual or bodily postures. These presuppositions can be summarised as the giving of one’s body over to the event. Put differently, the human experiences an embodied and dialogic relationship with the image, which is contextualised and dependent on a broader situation rooted in the physicality of the device and its meaning; i.e., sitting at a desk, turning on a computer, putting a VR headset on or pulling out a smartphone, are to get into position, to orient the screen in relation to the body and go through these pre-perceptual manoeuvres to put the body in an event. Inasmuch as humans are consumers, they allow their bodies to be consumed in order to consume. The device’s function in the event is to make a space for our bodies and the image is not in the device; the device, in itself, does not produce the image because the actualisation of an image-object depends on specific bodily and intellectual postures.
113
The body is not captured by realism attributed to an already fully emerged and existing image but is, rather, instrumentalised and made into an object. The device does not imitate styles from the natural world familiar to our sensory organs to give our bodies the experience of an image. Instead, an image-event occurs via the aforementioned embodied performance with the device. One does not see a tree outside only to later discover it is a rasterized image. A rasterized tree on a computer monitor could be mistaken for a photo, but the error lies in the assignment of the wrong imaging technology to a particular case; while the type of image can be brought into question, the fact that it is an image on a monitor cannot. This is because its status, as an image, emerges from a more basic presupposed condition, namely the event. Our bodies perform and perceive a tree as an event with a monitor before we contemplate how it was generated as an image. We can marvel at the photorealism of a rasterized tree but photorealism’s goal and its achievement is not to fool our bodies, though this is the project’s understanding of itself. From the perspective of lived performance, photorealism invites into the image-event an idea that can change the posture we assume in relation to our bodies. To celebrate photorealism is to applaud an image-object, but this framed object and the act of applause itself are parts of an event being brought into dialogic relation with other parts, particularly our bodies. We can demonstrate two levels from which to view photorealism, like this:
  • The image-event invites the formulation of an image-object, which is to give space for the following performance:
    • The image-object resembles something in the natural world, but this is an illusion, the body has been ‘tricked’.
The latter of these affirms the instrumentalisation of the body, leading to both its circumscription and the feeling of it being played with.
114
Below we will see how videogame graphics can demonstrate this more effectively than other imaging technologies because the videogame industry often values realism-enabling technologies, like ray tracing or path tracing, beyond aesthetics and for the sake of user participation. Ray tracing does not merely look good, it feels good to perform on/with the screen. The developer, player and device aspire to cast and trace some rays through a virtual world that is mutually dependent on all participants. We participate in this event with our bodies and unintentional but welcome, things are happening to our bodies — or to perception — because we are not the only actors. This experience relies on our regarding the event of image perception, both pre-reflectively and explicitly, in a particular way.
115
And this can show at the outset, before elaborating or revealing the transcendental nature of the videogame image, that computer graphics are not an illusion (which is obvious) but derive value from (1) the way they demonstrate popular Cartesian philosophical stances pertaining to the instrumentalisation of the body and (2) the way they make space for the body to participate in image-events, in particular ways which lead to valued events. These points, and the transcendental image, will receive more attention below, starting with what Merleau-Ponty (1945/2012:32) said concerning colour:
The first perception of colors, properly so called, is thus a change in the structure of consciousness, the institution of a new dimension of experience, and the deployment of an a priori.
And the same can be said of pixels performing said colour in new ways, like ray-traced reflections in Cyber Punk 2077 (Badowski et al. 2020):

After seeing this image, I can think about how it is being generated by a computer and can push to align my experience with that knowledge. That is, I can make an abstraction on top of the a priori given through perception, replacing the mountains, trees and clouds with an array of pixels changing colour. 117
While doing this can replace perceived images with pixels changing colour sixty times per second, this experience is an application of knowledge that steps over the simpler and embodied initial episode; it is not a true return to the base of things. We can break the image up, after the fact of perception on the device’s terms, with the device as our starting point, but this occludes the role of the body. Abstracting on the device’s terms is a similar operation to those performed earlier at 092 (observing) and 093 (onlooking), in that it displaces the image-object to a new location where it still functions as something we consume; the image is what these complex objects and events are here to provide. In contrast, our initial participation in the image-event, with our bodies and prior to conscious reflection, takes that experience as a whole in the first instance, and each successive frame is a new first instance. The consumed image is not something we assemble. The videogame image, like visual art, is seen before it is realised. This is unlike the image conceived as this website or a book or a videogame, i.e. a specific experience that must be read or played through, perhaps multiple times before we can orient ourselves in relation to its image or generate the whole image that is a work, for ourselves (see Merleau-Ponty 1945/2012:223). To consume an image is to assume it is all already there, ready to be eaten and we eat it in order to see it.

119
We gesture towards it with our eyes and our bodies while the image makes its own ‘first attack upon [our] senses’ (Merleau-Ponty 1945/2012:249), in a shared event. We act on the image and the image acts on us; we enter a mutual unfolding. We could say we become the image, display, apparatus, code, pixels, any element of the image-event, that these elements become something through us or we through them:
The thing and the world are given with the parts of my body, not through a ‘natural geometry’, but in a living connection comparable, or rather identical, to the living connection that exists among the parts of my body itself. (Merleau-Ponty 1945/2012:211)
For an image to be consumed, in the moment of perception it is not a separate object to look at. We do not think or assemble or deduce an image, we give ourselves, our bodies, to this new event or performance that is synonymous with its own discovery:
Myself as the one contemplating the blue of the sky is not an acosmic subject standing before it, I do not possess it in thought, I do not lay out in front of it an idea of blue that would give me its secret. Rather, I abandon myself to it, I plunge into this mystery, and it ‘thinks itself in me’. I am this sky that gathers together, composes itself, and begins to exist for itself, my consciousness is saturated by this unlimited blue. (Merleau-Ponty 1945/2012:222)
To see Cézanne’s (1905) brushstrokes again entails a removal of oneself from the whole and this removal is an additional reorientation away from that perceptual experience, an abstraction after the event of seeing. Likewise, on a computer screen, we cannot escape the final product and unsee a mountain at base; we can only add additional predicates to this new first experience. In this sense, the image has a certain hold on us and the event of seeing the image has changed us forever.
120
This process can be regarded as transcendental in that it is a condition of experience. To opt out of this event would be to never see what the image is of, to never click and reveal the image, undermining the intentionality that is the condition for its existence. Even the artist steps back once the picture is complete to appreciate the whole, to see it as a landscape and to behold what their process of abstraction has achieved, thereby giving themselves over to it. Now that its composition is complete and we behold it, the mountain or landscape will henceforth be the premise of all thought concerning the phenomenon. We have, as Merleau-Ponty (1945/2012:32) told us at 115, ‘the deployment of an a priori’.
121
Merleau-Ponty’s (1945/2012:13) assertion that ‘the perceived world is the always presupposed foundation of all rationality, all value and all existence’ applies equally to images when we regard them as things we encounter in the natural world and disregard their status as mediums containing produced content. For now, never-mind the medium, McLuhan (1964/2013), only look at the content. It arrives complete. And after a detailed exploration of the complex social, political and technological forces working to deliver that content, we look back at the image, but nothing has changed. We cannot not see the image because the real source of the image is the involuntary, pre-reflective, transcendental force of our bodies. The moment an image’s composition is complete, regardless of our previous dialogic (as we have described them) contributions to that process, the image is suddenly a new thing to be perceived. And the videogame is always a new image.
122
The videogame image, as transcendental image synonymous with the image-event, as novel perceptual event, precedes contextualisations in memory or conjecture (be it in relation to narrative elements or gameplay mechanics). We will hold these contextualisations as symptoms of a posture, which is pointed away from the lived event, that is, a way of regarding the videogame image which supposes an authority over terms and conditions belonging to the transcendental image as immanent performance. While the image-event can stimulate our recollection of broader circumstances and we can deliberately bring those circumstances to bear on our thoughtful and intentional participation in the performance of an image, this recall can feel like the removal of a part of ourselves from the image-event, or more precisely, in such instances our contribution to the event has fundamentally changed because the lived event has become an object for us, namely an object to comprehend or manipulate. The more we play a videogame and align ourselves with and contribute to the event it is predisposed towards, as an unfinished event, the less we abstract the image into an object and the more we unconsciously accept the image’s a priori status, the less we think as bodies in a chair and the more we think as an image or in the style the image-event needs us to think to fulfil our contribution, when we think at all — often the performance does not parent a thought.
123
This is why we can rarely recall specific runs in Spelunky 2 (Yu 2020), especially after playing for a while. Performing that type of image effectively involves leaning into the event. That is, giving more of our pre-reflective bodily states to it. Decisions are made on impulse to fulfil how the videogame image is wont to become. The event that is our person is becoming the image-event. For example, dodging arrows in Spelunky 2 is not a measured or calculated procedure but a spontaneous, evolving and creative emergence. We refer to the mode in which it is true that, in a very important sense, one feels the arrow move passed and miss the playable character, which is how we get a feel for the arrows and learn how to dodge them. Like Merleau-Ponty’s (1945/2012:144-145) description of a cane, the arrow’s speed is something you experiment with and get a feel for via the performance of it, as an extension of our bodies:
If I want to become habituated to a cane, I try it out, I touch some objects and, after some time, I have it ‘in hand’: I see which objects are ‘within reach’ or out of reach of my cane. This has nothing to do with a quick estimate or a comparison between the objective length of the cane and the objective distance of the goal to be reached. Habit expresses the power we have of dilating our being in the world, or of altering our existence through incorporating new instruments.
125
Our flow state with an image is possible because the image is not the product of some rationalism. It is an event happening to a device and we, as bodies, can become with that event and we habitually call this event ‘the image’. In this sense, it is not a ‘virtual’ event, in the way ‘virtual’ is referred to in ‘virtual reality’, but an event in the perceived world. We perceive the arrow in Spelunky 2 (Yu 2020) by performing it with our bodies and incorporating the playable character into the ‘voluminosity’ of our bodies.
126
Not all thought and conscious reflection within the image-event instrumentalises our bodies and generates an image-object. There are frequent moments where we think and reflect according to the image-event, on its terms so as not to fold back and circumscribe the source of the ‘image’. An instance of this was represented above, in the image of me throwing a dog at the arrow trap to set it off.
I did not say to myself, ‘I want the image on this monitor to resemble a cartoon dog getting impaled by an arrow, so I will push these keys on my keyboard in this particular way’, etc. My thought process was on the image’s terms, within the world of the image, direct to the ‘fiction’ being performed: ‘I see there’s a trap there — too risky to dodge or whip it — I’ll just throw the dog — it’ll probably survive’. And in instances like this, we are yet to perform an abstraction, we think with the image as if it were infinite and it is infinite via the participation of our bodies, inasmuch as the body is an infinite object. No thought escapes contributing to the event and the force of thought is only concerned with performance and does not bother to comprehend the whole of a circumstance. The event is its own representation or is not concerned with representation because it merely performs its own conditions as transcendental.
127
To say that the image is transcendental and a priori is not a reference to chronology. Something so obviously and intentionally planned and constructed as a videogame entails a process and history. However, these processes, when brought to bear on this lived, open and unresolved event in front of us, accounts for the videogame in a way that separates the human from its emergence in the instance of its happening. This way of framing the videogame fails to describe what the human being has and is becoming via their participation in the image-event. ‘Fictional’ contextualisations within the image itself, like the videogame’s story or mechanics, can be closer, but only while they are considered on the image-event’s terms, as we just saw.
128
The immanent, lived experience of videogames is involved in how videogames are made because the videogame image is always new. Playing a videogame is an inseparable part of its past and continuing development (including mods, updates, etc.). The developers had to labour over this, already happening, becoming, evolving image to produce a finished product, which we then purchase. The intention to play Spelunky 2 (Yu 2020), Street Fighter V (Nakayama 2016) or The Last of Us Part II (Druckmann et al. 2020) is to regard these experiences as objects but to play these games well, as videogames, these performances of historical contextualisation must make way for a new image. And it is in this mode of performing a new image, the present image, where the image is mere site for us to point our actions towards rather than a thing to comprehend, this is where the transcendental nature of the videogame image is acknowledged, where the human is performed and respected as irreducible.
129
We can regard all images as transcendental, as a priori sites of performation, not only digital images or the videogame image though they do constitute a special case as has been said. In the same way Merleau-Ponty’s (1945/2012) concepts apply to our entire experience of the perceived world and not only when we are thinking about the act of perception itself, abstracting an image-object. Notwithstanding this, there are aspects of the performance of images that are responsible for our regarding such events as images, which now ought to be further elucidated. To do this, we will regard the image in more general terms. We do not behold visual images — photographs, paintings, films or videogames — like we do other phenomena perceived in the natural world because this act of perception entails a different posture. To proceed further, this difference will need to be explored, non-specific to any medium, highlighting those aspects which set our performance of (in) an image apart. This will explain how images generate a body-subject while concealing other aspects of the event.
130
The image constitutes a distinct perceptual event, which points back to the process of perception and, in this way, it points back to our bodies as perceiving subjects. At 119 we used Merleau-Ponty’s (1945/2012:222) description of perceiving the sky and likened it to the experience of perceiving Cézanne’s (1905) painting or at 116 a rasterized mountain from The Witcher 3 (Badowski et al. 2020). But Merleau-Ponty was not describing the experience of perceiving an image, and while these examples sufficed for our above purposes, there remains a distinction to be drawn. An image of a mountain constitutes a different human event to the beholding of a real mountain because the image is dialogic in an inter-human sense, it has an authorship or otherness acknowledged in its classification as material.
A real mountain in the distance is a part of the world in the same way our bodies are a part of the world. Though our existence and the mountain’s share a mutuality, the latter communicates an indifference to us. An observer is not the subject of a far-off mountain, distorting the earth’s curvature, blocking the horizon — one can feel it do these things, one can become it being this way, but it is just as much unperceived.
131
The image, in contrast, is there for us and it wants to place us at the centre of perception; the perceiving body becomes the centre of a new event. The image generates a subject by virtue of its authorship. This is how Flusser (1983/2000:8) saw in the image, ‘a synthesis of two intentions: one manifested in the image and the other belonging to the observer’. The image carries its own intentionality; someone put it there. The viewer or user becomes the witness of something, they are the centre and the object of a process. The image was made to be seen and in as much as we reach out towards images, they reach back and communicate something about us as bodies and the fact that we behold an image.
Vain is the glory of the sky,
The beauty vain of field and grove,
Unless, while with admiring eye
We gaze, we also learn to love. (Wordsworth 1845/2004:186)
We are left with a profound image of the beauty of the sky, field, and grove, but, in truth, this image-object, generated through poetry to be aesthetically celebrated, is not real or transcendental. It emerged from the act of reading something that was given by Wordsworth (1845/2004:186), and any editors who may have contributed to presenting it to the reader. Here, we are describing a feature all images share, their potential to be abstracted as transcendent (not transcendental) authored objects subject to being thought at. Of course, there is a reality to the image but this does not reside where the discourse of aesthetics, philosophy, critical analysis, etc., points to, not the image they analyse. Rather, these modes of thought participate in the performance of the image, as event, as a consequence of their proximity to it. And this participation is necessarily occluded to accomplish their goals. But here we have different goals, in response to what we have discovered in the videogame, and the image we point to and perform in Videogames for a New Anthropological Image, is not something to think at, come to terms with or act on, rather it is a site or container which can enable new events, an organon which can leverage thought and human expression. Videogames are unique, as images, because they also perform this aspect of images explicitly. The videogame is both a whole, immediately perceived, and yet unfinished, a process that is waiting for us to make happen. This is true of all images, however, the image is accompanied by a mixture of alterity and containment. Alterity, because its mimesis is part of its essence, as an exercise in representation it must always be distinct from the world, or non-world as fiction, it represents. Containment, because, as a target for bodily orientation, though it might claim to represent the whole of human experience or the world — images of the philosophical type — it is encountered by the human within the world — on a screen, piece of paper, etc. So, the performance of this mixture necessarily presupposes an observer; we could also say it creates one.
132
In this, we see that the image-event is subjective because it generates a subject. It is an abstract hallucination to regard any image as objective, this includes photographs and digital images generally, and to regard an image as objective is to ignore the process of its emergence (Flusser 1983/2000:15). Images arrest and impose upon us a particular perspective, notwithstanding different interpretations. These differing interpretations all express the same fundamental orientation towards the image, they presuppose the stance of an interpreter. All interpretations of an image share the spatial alignment necessary to view the thing as an image. Merleau-Ponty’s (1945/2012:226) assertion that all perception is spatial is relevant here. The spatiality we experience in images is only possible as a bodily gesture, for ‘...there would be for me no such thing as space if I did not have a body’ (1945/2012:104). The image literally puts our bodies in a physical place. We have seen this place transform across mediums as they each introduce a different ‘modality of this [the body’s] anchorage’ (Merleau-Ponty 1945/2012:296), from the painting to the camera with its new forms of resemblance to the cinema (as we have seen in Deleuze’s (1983/2013:24) movement-image) to the videogame image where we enter a new space, uniquely unfolding into a more totalising encompassment of our bodies, of a world, via dialogic inter-human situations, explicit questions awaiting us within the image, i.e. interactivity. The videogame is a new night: ‘The night is not an object in front of me; rather, it envelops me, it penetrates me... it is from within nocturnal space that I unite with it’ (Merleau-Ponty 1945/2012:296). Thus with each new medium we are dealing with radically new bodily situations and new worlds — for ‘we are our world’ and ‘my body is the pivot of the world’ (Merleau-Ponty 1945/2012:84, 109). Thus, the image entails a very specific, embodied and subjective experience, which could be described as a new world, only life is full of new worlds and this is not a specific feature of images. ‘New worlds’ is what the body does; it is constantly generating new worlds. Images can orient the body in such a way that it no longer wants to claim this function for itself. The image-object orientation puts the new world in this local limited finite space to be perceived, inviting a posture where the body is no longer something we perform but something which consumes or receives stimuli — stimuli for subjects to interpret or produce.
133
So when new image technologies emerge, rather than generating novel accurate representations of the world in greater detail, they signify new perceptual situations, new body states, gestures and habits for us to become used to. This is why our initial reactions to new imaging technologies never last. We become practised at getting our grip on the situations until they are seamlessly incorporated into the playspace of our being-in-the-world. Dauthendey (cited in Benjamin 1931/2015:69) describes this in relation to people’s first encounters with photographs:
In the early days, people did not dare to look for very long at the first pictures [Daguerre] produced. They were startled by the vividness of the figures and believed that the tiny little faces of the personages who appeared on the image could see them too. That is how uncanny an effect the unaccustomed vividness and lifelikeness of the first daguerreotype images exerted on everyone.
134
The novelty of images as new orientations for our bodies is also a source of great appeal. We see this in the videogame industry’s preoccupation with graphical photorealism and how arresting the application of new technologies can be for us. What now seems like an exaggerated hyperbole, was Kasavin (1997:para.2) expressing his genuine experience of Final Fantasy VII (Kitase 1997) in his 1997 review:
This is the most dazzling visual experience to date on any console. Film-quality computer-generated cinematics blend seamlessly with pre-rendered background artwork to create the strikingly realistic world of Final Fantasy VII, both beautiful in its grandeur and terrifying in its detail.
135
Our experience with images can change radically over time, not because some new element of the image is discovered, or greater realism achieved, but because our position in relation to images generally, the habits of our bodies, is constantly changing. In this way, we do not encounter images the same way we encounter other objects in the natural world. An image-event occurs when we contract our being, our body, our capacity for there to be a world, into a single point. We apply that capacity onto a canvas, which is what gives images spatiality. Our world and spatiality are given to the image-event and, in this sense, rather than being a thing in the natural world, we can regard the image to be our body’s performance of a novel event.
136
Contra Dauthendey’s (cited in Benjamin 1931/2015:69) disturbed participants in the first photographs, who rapidly became accustomed to the new medium, our reactions to the videogame image are frequently refreshed because the image is tied to technological developments which strive for novelty and realism. Today, the phenomenon of the photograph is unremarkable. The event did not disturb or surprise humans for long and we can attribute this fast habituation to the simplicity of the apparatus producing or preserving the image. As Flusser (1983/2000:16) points out, the apparatus of the camera itself indeed harbours some mystery, in this way, the process of generating the photograph can remain hidden, but the photo itself, in one’s hand, is easy to get to the bottom of and see the process by which it is presently manifest. One can fold up a photograph and put it in a pocket and a painting can be comprehended similarly; the brush strokes are visible on close, secondary inspection – the thing has been conquered, there is no further mystery. Notwithstanding this simplicity, the photograph or perspectivism in painting has dramatically altered the way humans understand their place in the world. For example, Flusser (1983/2000:19-20) has explored this in relation to history and Taylor (2007:144) described perspectivism’s role in the emergence of providential deism. However, it is more difficult to comprehend what we have given to the experience of the monitor or more specifically the videogame to make it possible; we see the pixels, but they keep changing, even in accordance with our bodies as we move the stick on a controller. It is impossible to consistently comprehend the complex origins of the rasterized mountain on a computer screen. This is how, unlike a painting or photograph, we are closed off from the genesis of the hold the rasterized image has on us in the very moment we experience it. The continuous process of the monitor’s cycles and the computer attached to it have become too complex for us to keep track of. In this way, the computer is performing the conditions of the immediately given and our bodies cannot outrun the mystery of the machine. We enter a perception event and elements of that event are ultimately prior to any conscious reflections about it. This is how the digital image can deploy a new a priori with features that demand an explanation. This discourse of explanations, comprehending how the technology works and manipulates our bodies as objects, is an empowering process and part of the technology’s appeal.
137
There are aspects of the videogame image that do not contribute to the image’s ‘realism’. Rather, these aspects reveal a style, which is tied to how the image was technologically produced. For example, the strangeness of said mountain: the sharp lines and odd lighting which make it distinct from a photograph of a mountain, as well as the flatness, smallness and lack of bodily presence found in most images (virtual reality is an exception). The videogame image is contained in a rectangle that is clearly nested or anchored in the natural world but its distinct style comes from limitations tied to its need to be generated in real time. The image is not just happening to us, we are also happening to the image in the event of gameplay. This unresolved aspect of interactive images has, so far with current technology, necessitated particular styles.
138
These features or ‘flaws’ do not prevent us from experiencing the image as fully as a photorealistic one, but they help us identify the type of image we see, and signal, culturally, how we ought to talk about and judge it. Dwelling on the particularities of a rasterized image, for example, only describes the type of experience we are having and does nothing to weaken the hold the image has on our bodies in that first instance of our perceiving it.
139
We experience the image with/as bodies and because our bodies perform a limitless world, the body does not impose limits on the image. After this infinite object, namely the body, is given to generate an experience, thought enters to frame a finite plane separate from our bodies, namely the image. What manifests itself as the distinct style of the videogame in our thinking about it (e.g. broken textures, lighting or artefacts), is a symptom of our performed empowerment and circumscription of the situation. Importantly, this feeling of domination is tied to our active involvement in, and transformation of, the experience of the image.
140
Realism, as a value judgement placed on images, comes to bear after the event of initial perception, in the event of further abstractions, explanations, the tracing of the image’s history in relation to its production, etc. We pit this thought exercise against our more fundamental submission to the image, which is our concession in the act of looking at it, we gave it our bodies.
Our first perception of an image is not a hallucination, but the dominance secondary abstractions come to philosophically impose on human experience, and the practice of making those value judgements is hallucinatory.
 The image has a different first instance to a ‘real’ mountain but both are true experiences and not the product of abstract thought.
And abstract thought is also a true experience though not necessarily in the way it assumes itself to be and not in a way that allows it to penetrate and reduce the experience of beholding an image.
 But knowing the image’s reality is different, and analysing the image on those terms according to that difference, does nothing to negate one’s body’s participation in the image-event; philosophers and appraisers of the image, still experience the image because the determined ‘realness’ of it is not what captures us. Many of us enjoy participating in images for the way they are not ‘real’ and the dominating stance this enables us to assume in relation to ‘images’ — more precisely image-events — but this domination happens when we augment the event of perceiving an image with thoughts external to what is necessary for us to participate in it.
141
We can describe this image as a broken mountain and show how it is undone with the pulling of a thread, leaning in to see the individual pixels or the unrealistic distribution of light. But doing this entails ignoring the image-event and paying no heed to the next instance which always provides a new image.
142
Of course, we never mistake an image for occurrences in the natural world, but again, that is a matter of difference and not legitimacy. We experience images as images in the first instance and are never trapped inside the ‘matrix’ (Wachowski and Wachowski 1999). The experience’s status as image and the conditions of its production are presupposed and understood in the act of directing our bodies towards it. But these very thoughts and the framing of an image as an object, these preceding contextualisations are present before the image is consumed in the same way subsequent abstractions are. The experience of the image, the real image-event in its radically immanent and immediate form, these questions of ‘realism’ and Cartesian (Descrates 1641/2003:22) nightmares cannot be present, there is no room for them, they are incomprehensible. This includes aesthetic discussions grounded in any medium whatever but also videogames, particularly criticisms pertaining to the trajectory of videogame graphics towards photorealism. Consider the latter: the appraisal of videogame graphics necessarily construes the videogame image as an illusion, and this illusion is rationalised away in the assumption of a posture that can assess it. This posture is incapable of acknowledging the pre-reflective space that is the contribution of our bodies to the event. We ignore what the image-event is doing to our bodies, how it is orienting us and what we are contributing to that event and, rather, focus on the image as an object that is seeking to align with the natural world. When the videogame image is judged in this way, it ceases to be a dialogic image, an unresolved event to which we, as performing bodies, perform or contribute. In this mode, the true videogame image is hidden from us. We are unaware of our participation in the image-event, we naively construct, we auto-position, an image-object. As we have seen, there is a necessary mutuality to the production of a videogame image, but nothing in the event depends on it being framed as an image via the assumption of a position external to it.
This is because the act of playing a videogame presupposes our accepting that the image is ‘determined-in-the-last-instance’ (see Laruelle 1996/2013:125).

Abstract, theoretical encompassment can only happen after the image has already been perceived, and because each consecutive moment gives us a new potential image-object, this style of thought finds itself being constantly overtaken by real human experience. The above descriptions of the role of the body, as situation, in the image-event that is a videogame is not an obvious one, even for phenomenologists, notwithstanding the role Merleau-Ponty’s (1945/2012) work played in inspiring it — and we will not let that constrain what we do with it. We see this in how the phenomenological tradition has been applied to digital media more generally. It has been made clear above, using Merleau-Ponty (1945/2012:355), that we participate in the experience of the image by incorporating it into the experience of our bodies; ‘the body is the situation’. In the same way, perception is never isolated to a single sensory organ, the entire body is geared towards phenomena by becoming an event with it. When discussing computers, there is a tendency to exclude the body from an analysis of what is transpiring on the screen. Dreyfus (2009) famously employed Heideggerian philosophy to critique artificial intelligence and the internet, but this was mostly to bring two different understandings of reality up against each other. In this way, Dreyfus’ work, importantly, has thrown into relief assumptions about human intelligence and the world, tied to a rationalism that is foundational to the development of new technologies. But the development of artificial intelligence is not a phenomenological issue. To make it so, we must contextualise it as a manifestation of the philosophical traditions phenomenology was a reaction against – the philosopher needs an interlocutor.

A philosopher could do the same thing with the videogame image, framing its pursuit of graphical realism as philosophical demonstration; it has conquered perception with pure maths and physics and — to use Wittgenstein’s (1953/2009:53e) phrase — ‘a picture held us captive’. The popularity of simulation arguments based on projections of a virtual world indistinguishable from reality demonstrates the potency of this image and makes this association easy. Once this framing is set, computer-generated images can be critiqued with any philosophy critical of Descartes (1641/2003), especially Heideggerian existentialism or phenomenology.

A philosophical critique can detract from the value of the digital world, as Dreyfus (2009) has shown, and one can use Merleau-Ponty (1945/2012) to vouch for the primacy of the body in these times of digital selves. These are the obvious philosophical paths to take in response to what recent technologies are accomplishing, to regard and respond to them as philosophical demonstrations of rationalist ideas. But this does nothing to think along with the event as a situated phenomenon. We have been preparing a view of the videogame image in which the normative discourse of ‘computer graphics’ is made subordinate, as incidental interior element, to the image-event itself. The posture we assume while playing a videogame, which is the phenomena’s invitation to us, is non-philosophical. We have discovered this through participation in the lived event and through a determination to make space for it, to uncover a style of thought belonging to the videogame. This is opposed to the generation of a philosophical interlocutor via a hidden decision to account for everything.

So rather than a philosophical engagement that happens to the videogame, we can give the videogame a new anthropological image. ‘New’, because it is emerging, interactive and unfinished like the videogame; ‘anthropological’ for its capacity to be non-philosophical like the videogame; ‘image’, to subvert the term while extending the practice’s application to all image-events. This new location for philosophical discourse within our image of the experience of an image liberates the videogame as human practice. Any limitations placed on the videogame image are additions that distance us from the initial human dialogic experience. The site of the image-event is a finite object and the image on our screen has a very specific and limited intentionality, as something which is authored, but these features are not experienced as limitations on the level of interactivity, which is how we think according to, and along with, the videogame as we perform it. There, we become the image. It is not something to think about philosophically, but something to participate in, and the most we can do is observe that participation by taking it up as new practice.

All postures, philosophical or otherwise, presuppose the videogame as a world. At 116, we considered how the rasterized videogame image’s eccentricities invite an unveiling, that is, a critique or contemplation or simple awareness of the image’s history and how it was produced. But an unlimited, perceived world at the site of the image is presupposed for this to occur. Not a fictional world, but an infinite plane of intellectual and bodily activity. This mode stops explicitly ‘believing’ in the image-event as a world, as an inexhaustible site for human expression, to instead have faith in its ability to disassemble an image-object. But to play a videogame, one has faith in the inter-human event itself. The parameters of the body, the difference between oneself and the image, are not drawn here, for the body is surrendered over to that event. Merleau-Ponty (1945/2012:5) regards perception to be an expression of primordial belief, that is, a faith or expectation in the world prior to thought. The baby extends their arm to grasp the object, not because their brain harbours some pre-configured map of the world, and neither is it the event of imagination based on a priori categories (Kant 1781/2007), but it is based on a kind of impulsive faith, not in anything specific, just in the fact that there is anything at all. A videogame is an image that entails this same unlimited gesture, but which no longer imposes on itself the status: representation (unlike other forms of the image).

Any successful image of the videogame must be open in the same way a videogame is. A concern like this generates a comparison between the different sites of potential action, in a register completely foreign to the experience of a videogame, ignoring the dialogic in-between-ness. We do not dismiss this and similar issues lightly when choosing to stay on target, which is the image-event itself, not reflections that only become possible before or after that event. Presenting the CRT image as Difference

The CRT image is experienced and lived in a way that is fundamentally different from the LCD image and there are some technical reasons for this, which we can now elucidate.

Many passionate online communities who prefer CRTs express their preferences on the grounds of image quality alone. The CRT has certain inherent features which LCD manufacturers still often struggle to achieve. CRTs can have a higher perceived dynamic range; colours are richer and potentially more accurate; because CRTs have no set number of pixels, they can scale to a larger variety of resolutions and do so natively; images are softer, but this also means lower resolutions look better; most of all, because the image is formed by an electron beam rapidly scanning a phosphorescent screen, entire frames cannot bleed into each other, there is no pixel response time, therefore motion clarity is perfect. Recent LCD technology called ‘black frame insertion’ or ‘back-light strobing’, attempts to recreate this effect in LCDs, often at the cost of reduced brightness. Of course, nostalgia plays a role in CRT’s appeal today too, especially for playing retro PC games.

The CRT, as an artefact today, offers us the participation in a different philosophical stance because we are reversing technology’s ‘progress’, reducing the image. Its size is smaller with less overall clarity. The apparatus does not form to the shape of the body, but one is forced to navigate and compensate for it with one’s body. This all allows us to see the qualitative difference in image style as it affects the image-event. The CRT-image is grounded in a weighted physicality, there is depth to it that aligns with the depth of the machine itself. ‘It looks like you’re looking through a lens, like a piece of glass instead of a digital display’ (Linneman 2019:5’28”) and more than that, in a way that feels like you are looking into another world. The rounded glass at the front of the device is looked through into a beyond that has physical presence and which is unknown, lending mystery to the image. The size and weight of the monitor manifest themselves in one’s immediate perception of the image being produced; the electrons fired from the cathode pass through a physically hidden cavity, which our bodies enter into a relation with. What is a nihilism in the flat Samsung panel, seeking to conceal all its inner workings in order to give our bodies an isolated image, becomes mystery that has meaning in the CRT for the body and its relation to the world.

Thus we can think against the direction of technological development, against the removal of meaning from the embodied experience of the image. We can recontextualise perception, via different situations inspiring different orientations to the image.

So here, discourse of the image is not yet fully subsumed into the event, we do not yet regard a unified phenomena where the videogame and our current practice, namely talking about the videogame, can be simultaneously regarded in a ‘vision-in-one’ (Laruelle 1996/2013).
176
But the need for this vision and a striving for it continues to arise: at 127, so that the transcendental image is causally uncoupled from descriptions of an image’s origins, at 129, so that an event’s pre-reflective categorisation as ‘image’ is revealed to be an aspect of its performance and at 163, so that this text is performatively equated with previously captured videogame footage, it does not surpass it with a capacity to better describe or understand the phenomena but is rather an instance of the phenomena.
177
The gesture of 163 reaches the vision, but the problem of how to proceed from there remains. In an attempt to address this, 164 aligns with the theme of display technologies and reframes the challenges of realism and immersion, positioning them as aspects of the event rather than objects of striving. This approach is embodied in 164’s description of the CRT image-event.
178
Thus, 165 regards the transcendental image-event as a phenomenon that includes interpretations of it as potential constituents of it. So, we are no longer resisting the notion of an image-object, but take the image-object as a feature of performance. We assume a posture that does not reject representation, for we represent things because of the radically immanent situation, determined in the last instance, not for a supposed circumscription of the real. This is how we play videogames.
179
If we accept what the videogame never ceases to demonstrate — that is, as an image, it is its own best representation of itself — rather than using that to generate a space for simplicity or inaccessibility within a new philosophy of videogames (which is really a reduction and a dismissal of the event), we can actually follow through and receive what is discovered in the videogame itself as an invitation. This is not to capture the videogame event, but rather to generate a new image-event of our own. Videogames for a New Anthropological Image commenced in pursuit of conclusions, these were encountered early for they are easy to find, but the videogame demands more from us, namely the dispensing of conclusions in favour of a new image.
180
The videogame is not essential to this posture but our particular style and our discovery of it on this occasion is thanks to the videogame. This not only refers to how the image is played and interacted with in a way that conveys some semblance of videogames but the movements of ideas. This videogame style of thought does not entail the distanced deployment of concepts from an external relativism, because each position — as they were represented by 167-178 — involves an actioning commitment and situational belief in the occasion of the movement; each force (of) thought can be locally legitimised by its circumstance, its truth can be measured according to its role in the performance of thought. Videogames also entail and enjoy this kind of ‘relative autonomy’ (Laruelle 1996/2013); each videogame runs according to its own rules and the value of gestures is determined via the presupposition of those rules — as we have seen in Street Fighter. Videogames can be playful in this way because no one takes them seriously, which in no way undermines the meaningfulness and gravity of their contributions to thought and human experience. So philosophy and anthropology, and any thought striving for sophistication, deepness and a usefulness as representation, notwithstanding some historical hubristic baggage and ambition, can enjoy a relative autonomy in the same way videogames do if we take them all less seriously. This increased levity would mean, we do not give those thought practices power over us, we do not let them determine us or our performance of them, or determine our creative response to the experience of them, but let us determine them unidirectionally, as we find ourselves determined in the last instance according to the real.
181
We perform this project as a videogame, as it appears, as it is played, as it is felt and experienced with our bodies, in our lounge rooms, on the CRT I found by the side of the road, in my analysis of that experience, during the fact and after the fact, in a new image where the original is not only historically referred to but is equally part of this new image, determined in the last instance. We can put these aspects, including the videogame and whatever forces moved me to pursue it as a research topic, in the immediate lived event, but not as context. And the event is not derivative or dependent on such elements, rather, they come out of the image-event, as real, as irreducible unframed human event. This is the posture one assumes to play a videogame, which is a transcendental image.
